How to explain fractions simply

A fraction is just fair sharing written down. 3/4 means: 3 candy bars, 4 kids, everyone gets the same — each kid's share is "three fourths."

The rule that does the work

Kids meet sharing years before they meet fractions, so start where they already live. The bottom number is how many kids are sharing. The top number is how many things there are to share. That's it — every fraction they'll ever see is some version of that sentence. 1/2 is one sandwich, two kids. 3/4 is three candy bars, four kids. 7/1 is seven candy bars, one very happy kid.

The words that matter (and plain versions)

Numerator — the top number. Plain version: "how many things we're sharing." Memory hook: number of things.

Denominator — the bottom number. Plain version: "how many dudes are sharing." It sounds silly, which is exactly why nobody forgets it.

Equivalent fractions — different writings of the same share. 1/2 and 2/4 are the same amount of sandwich; there are just more cuts. Cutting a sandwich into more pieces doesn't make more sandwich.

Where kids actually get stuck

"Bigger bottom number means bigger piece." Their whole-number instincts say 8 > 4, so 1/8 > 1/4. The candy bar fixes it: would you rather split one bar with 4 kids or with 8? More kids, smaller piece.

Treating the two numbers separately. A fraction is one amount, not two numbers sitting near each other. Keep saying "each kid's share" — one thing, not two.

Fractions bigger than 1 feel illegal. 5/4 isn't weird: five candy bars, four kids — everyone gets a whole bar and a bit more. That "bit more" moment makes improper fractions boring instead of scary.

Say it like this in class

"Every fraction you'll ever see is answering one question: if we share this fairly, how much does each person get? Bottom number — how many people. Top number — how many things. The line just means 'shared between.'"
