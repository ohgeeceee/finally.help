How to explain photosynthesis simply

Plants cook their own food. Sunlight is the stove, water and air are the ingredients, sugar is the meal — and oxygen is the smell drifting out of the kitchen.

The analogy that does the work

Tell students a leaf is a tiny kitchen. The plant can't go to the store, so it cooks everything it eats from scratch. Sunlight is the heat — no stove, no cooking. Water comes up from the roots like a pipe to the sink, and carbon dioxide floats in from the air like an ingredient that walks in by itself. The plant mixes them, cooks them with sunlight, and out comes sugar — its food. The leftover steam from all that cooking is oxygen, which the plant pushes out and we get to breathe.

The words that matter (and plain versions)

Chlorophyll — the green stuff in leaves. Say it like this: "the pot the plant cooks in." It's what catches the sunlight, and it's why plants are green.

Carbon dioxide — the part of the air the plant breathes in. Students already know it as "what we breathe out," which makes the plant/person swap instantly memorable.

Glucose — a fancy word for the sugar the plant makes. You can just say "plant food" until the vocabulary test.

Where textbooks lose kids

Starting with the formula. 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ means nothing until the kitchen story exists. Story first, formula after.

"Energy conversion." True, but abstract. "The stove cooks the food" lands better than "light energy is converted to chemical energy" — save the upgrade for the second pass.

Skipping why it matters to us. The oxygen leftover is the punchline: every breath you take is a plant's kitchen exhaust. Don't bury it.

Say it like this in class

"Raise your hand if you cooked today. None of you? A plant cooked. It made its own breakfast using sunlight as a stove, water from its roots, and a gas from the air — and the only thing it threw out is the oxygen you're breathing right now."
